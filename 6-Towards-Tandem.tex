With the recent world-record efficiency of $29.5 ~\%$ for a perovskite/silicon tandem \cite{NREL.2021} exceeding the theoretical efficiency limit of $29.4 ~\%$ for a Si single-junction \cite{Richter.2013}, tandem solar cells are getting more and more attention from the PV community. The combination of a perovskite cell on top of a \ac{c-Si} bottom cell is thereby especially attractive, as it promises high efficiency at affordable costs \cite{Green.2013,Sofia.2019,Messmer.2020c}. %ausfürhlicher? vorteile von Si und pero nochmal deutlicher ansprechen...nee, nicht nötig!
For straightforward module and system integration, the monolithic two-terminal tandem configuration is preferred. %cite? wohl eher nicht, nichts gefunden...

Such a configuration requires low-ohmic and transparent interconnection of both sub-cells.

An alternative are Si-based tunneling junctions (SiTJ), as first demonstrated in a perovskite/Si tandem cell by Mailoa \textit{et al.} \cite{Mailoa.2015}, which have the benefit of better refractive index matching between and thus reduce reflection losses \cite{Grant.2016}. Additionally, they have the potential to be directly integrated in the processing of the bottom cell's passivating contact \cite{Luderer.2019,Sahli.2017,Sahli.2018b,Peibst.2019,Puaud.2021}. 

Looking at recent milestones in perovskite/Si tandem development, like the fully textured tandem presented by Sahli \textit{et al.} \cite{Sahli.2018b} or the (at that time) world-record setting device by Al-Ashouri \textit{et al.} \cite{Al-Ashouri.2020}, it is apparent that SHJ-based bottom cells are most commonly used for high-efficiency lab-scale tandems.\footnotemark\footnotetext{Unfortunately for the current world-record perovskite/Si tandem solar cell no details about the utilized Si bottom cell or any process details in general have been published.}  %stimmt das?
Reasons for that are the outstandingly high $V_{oc}$ of SHJ and suitable optics as a bottom cell, as for a-Si:H the poor blue response is irrelevant in a tandem and the \ac{FCA} in the relevant infrared region is negligible.
However, as stated by Messmer \textit{et al.}, "it is an open issue of how to transfer these
research results to industrial mass production, which is driven by cost reduction and
resource efficiency and includes challenges like up-scaling and long-term stability" \cite{Messmer.2020c}. Sofia \textit{et al.} even pointed out a "clear mismatch between commercial viability and current research efforts" and motivated the pursuit of low-cost
Si bottom cells in perovskite/Si tandems as they enable greater reductions in \ac{LCOE} compared to higher-efficiency but also higher-cost Si bottom cells \cite{Sofia.2019}.

Compared to SHJ-based bottom cells, poly-Si-based passivating contacts have the benefit of higher thermal stability, making them compatible with the mainstream Si solar cell structure, i.e., the PERC technology \cite{ITRPV.2020}. Further, a temperature stable bottom cell might be preferable for future application, as lead-free perovskite absorbers are supposed to need annealing temperatures exceeding the thermal stability of SHJ bottom cells \cite{Yang.2020}. % mehr zitieren? To profit from the industrial viability of $p$-type PERC cells and the good optical
and electrical properties of the established PERC rear side \cite{Messmer.2020c}, an integration of this cell design is highly desirable \cite{Peibst.2019}. For the simplest approach, an unpassivated phosphorus diffused $n^{++}$ emitter could be contacted directly on full-area, but this would result in high recombination losses at the unpassivated surface. Another approach is the local opening of the dielectric front side passivation layer to balance recombination in the contacted (unpassivated) emitter region and low-resistive cell interconnection \cite{Wu.2017} (Fig. \ref{fig:PERC}). An option, not limited by such geometrical constraints is the use of a full-area passivating contact as replacement for the $n^{++}$ emitter in combination with a full-area TCO or a Si thin film for electrical and optical top and bottom cell interconnection.
A particularly attractive approach is a tunnel oxide based passivating front contact combined with the well-established rear side of the PERC technology ("TOPerc") \cite{Peibst.2019,Messmer.2020c}. 
Similar to the approach of using TOPCon-based rear contacts \cite{Feldmann.2020,Richter.2021} this can be seen as another option to lower recombination losses of silicon based solar cells by passivating contacts.
Either a TCO ("ReCo-TOPerc", Fig. \ref{fig:ITO}) or a TCO-free $p^+$/$n^+$ poly-SiTJ \cite{Peibst.2019,Luderer.2019} ("SiTJ-TOPerc", Fig. \ref{fig:TJ}) could serve as interconnection layer between both sub-cells, enabling efficient transport of the sub-cells' majority carriers.

According to simulations perovskite/Si tandems utilizing TOPerc bottom cells promise high efficiencies comparable with SHJ %(or TOPCon$^2$) 
tandems at slightly lower all-in cell costs \cite{Messmer.2020c}.

In this chapter first ReCo-TOPerc solar cell results showing high $V_{oc}$ and low $R_s$ will be presented. A $p^+$/$n^+$ poly-SiTJ compatible with the PERC technology, enabling low $\rho_c$ and high i$V_{oc}$ at the same time will be developed and investigated thoroughly in the next chapter.

For the TOPerc concept the compatibility of poly-Si/SiO$_x$ passivating contacts (TOPCon) with the PERC process route, and in particular with the fast thermal process\footnotemark\footnotetext{This process is often referred to as "firing". In this thesis the term rapid thermal process (RTP) is used.} necessary for the PERC rear contact formation, has to be tested first. Therefore, symmetrical planar poly-Si($n^+$)/SiO$_x$ test structures (as in Fig. \ref{fig:reference}, without the metal contacts) were utilized and exposed to an \ac{RTP}. Some of the samples received the furnace anneal (FA) prior to the RTP (FA+RTP) to form the contact to the absorber. For other samples this FA was omitted and the activation of the poly-Si($n^+$) and the contact formation to the absorber was realized solely during the RTP, mimicking the firing step of the Al back-surface-field (BSF) alloying (RTP-only, see routes in Fig. \ref{fig:TJ-ReCo-routes}). In Fig. \ref{fig:QSSPC-overview} the i$V_{oc}$ at 1 sun illumination of these samples is depicted as a figure of merit for the passivation quality in dependence of the set RTP peak temperature $T_{RTP}$. After FA and prior to RTP the i$V_{oc}$ was around $720-730 ~\textrm{mV}$, depending on the doping level within poly-Si($n^+$) (dark gray area). After RTP in the industrial belt furnace FFO the i$V_{oc}$ was reduced slightly with increasing $T_{RTP}$. At $T_{RTP} = 750 ~\textrm{°C}$ and $T_{RTP} = 900 ~\textrm{°C}$ the i$V_{oc}$ was 724 and 714 mV, respectively. Common $T_{RTP}$ for PERC solar cells are well within this range. Thus, the poly-Si($n^+$)/SiO$_x$ passivating contact provided good surface passivation after RTP without the need of an additional hydrogenation step. Such a hydrogenation step (here RPHP for 30 min at 400 °C) improved the i$V_{oc}$ of the FA+RTP route samples above 730 mV, slightly below standard TOPCon samples after hydrogenation without the RTP after the FA (hatched light gray area).
After RTP at $T_{RTP} = 1000 ~\textrm{°C}$, the upper limit of $T_{RTP}$ in the FFO, the i$V_{oc}$ dropped to $\approx 680 ~\textrm{mV}$.

The i$V_{oc}$ directly after RTP was rather poor (i$V_{oc} \le 670 ~\textrm{mV}$). Presumably, the fast temperature ramps of the RTP induced stress within
the layer stack due to different thermal expansion coefficients of poly-Si, SiO$_x$ and c-Si \cite{Tada.2000}. This increased the defect density at the SiO$_x$/c-Si interface, %cite hollemann sipv 21
which in turn reduced the passivation quality. Whereas lower-doped structures were
completely depassivated after RTP (i$V_{oc} < 600 ~\textrm{mV}$, $\delta = \textrm{low}$), the very high net doping concentration
in poly-Si($n^+$) with higher phosphorus doping preserved
some level of surface passivation (i$V_{oc} \approx 670 ~\textrm{mV}$, $\delta = \textrm{high}$) due to (field-effect)
screening of minority charge carriers. 
The i$V_{oc}$ markedly increased up to $\approx 715 ~\textrm{mV}$ after hotplate annealing at $425 ~\textrm{°C}$ for $30 ~\textrm{min}$ in ambient air (Appendix \ref{sec:SI}, Fig. \ref{fig:QSSPC-TJ-Hotplate-effect}). RTP-only structures showed poor passivation directly after RTP (i$V_{oc} \le 625 ~\textrm{mV}$), which was further decreased with increasing $T_{RTP}$ (green closed symbols). Dopant diffusion through the thermally grown SiO$_x$ into the c-Si wafer is inefficient with the thermal budget applied here \cite{Feldmann.2019}. 
This results in a less pronounced diffusion tail and less "relaxation of the SiO$_x$/c-Si interface" which is detrimental regarding both minority carrier recombination and majority carrier transport.
RTP-only structures are therefore even more reliant on a low interface state density, which is, apparently, not the case directly after RTP. 
RTP-only is made possible, however, with a hydrogenation step. After RPHP an i$V_{oc}$ of up to  $\approx 730 ~\textrm{mV}$ was obtained (open green and yellow symbols), similar compared to route FA+RTP. %ffo proben hiern icht so gut, temp zu niedrig! vor allem deutlich bei 750 °C set!!
It is worth mentioning that this hydrogenation might also be possible via the TCO deposition if a hydrogenated TCO is used, as shown by Tutsch \textit{et al.} \cite{Tutsch.2020b}.




Not only low recombination but also a low resistivity for
the majority carrier transport has to be guaranteed in a passivating contact \cite{Hermle.2020}. For the FA+RTP process route this is ensured by the FA. $\rho_{c}$ was low ($< 10 ~\textrm{m}\upOmega\textrm{cm}^2$) and independent of $T_{RTP}$ for the process route including the FA (not shown).
%erwähnen, dass hier immer hf-dip vor metallisierung? sollte eigentlich auch so klar sein...
For the RTP-only approach, without the FA, low-ohmic contact formation to the wafer is, however, challenging. In Fig. \ref{fig:rhoc-vs-temp} $\rho_c$ extracted from symmetric poly-Si($n^+$)/SiO$_x$/c-Si RTP-only structures is depicted in dependence of $T_{RTP}$. At lower $T_{RTP}$ the thermal budget was neither sufficient to provide enough activation of dopants within the poly-Si($n^+$) and in-diffusion of dopants into the c-Si wafer nor to generate a sufficient pinhole density in the oxide to allow efficient current transport. The $J-V$ characteristic resembled the one of back-to-back connected diodes \cite{Elhadidy.2012}, thus, the poly-Si/SiO$_x$ contact on both sides of the structure represented a Schottky contact with diode-like behavior (Appendix \ref{sec:SI}, Fig. \ref{fig:I-V-n-only-Schottky}). Consequently, $\rho_c$ was very high ($\rho_c > 10^3 ~\textrm{m}\upOmega\textrm{cm}^2$). With increasing $T_{RTP}$ $\rho_c$ decreased but was still high at highest $T_{RTP} = 950 ~\textrm{°C}$ ($\rho_c \approx 450 ~\textrm{m}\upOmega\textrm{cm}^2$), using SiO$_x$ thermally grown at $600 ~\textrm{°C}$ for $10 ~\textrm{min}$. 

To ease contact formation, a wet-chemically grown (HNO$_3$) oxide was used instead for the RTP-only route.
The HNO$_3$ oxide was slightly thinner (1.1 compared to 1.2 nm) and is more permeable for dopants because of lower molar fraction of stoichiometric SiO$_2$ (Si$^{4+}$) compared to thermally grown SiO$_x$, as measured via \ac{XPS} by Polzin \textit{et al.} \cite{Polzin.2020}.\footnotemark\footnotetext{Different Si tunneling oxide for application in a passivating contact are currently investigated in the doctoral thesis of Jana Polzin, a colleague of the author.}
The sub-stoichiometry also eases oxide break-up upon thermal treatment \cite{Moldovan.2015}, resulting in a higher pinhole density. For these reasons, a low-resistive contact to the wafer was ensured due to efficient transport via pinholes \cite{Peibst.2016} and possibly an increased tunneling probability resulting from enhanced dopant diffusion \cite{Steinkemper.2015} across the HNO$_3$ oxide compared to the thermal oxide. %wirklich mit diffusion schreiben?

Ingenito \textit{et al.} had to introduce small amounts of carbon (C, $\approx 2.5 ~\textrm{at}\%$) to their Si layer to avoid delamination upon RTP, so-called blistering \cite{Ingenito.2018}. Note that this was not necessary for the poly-Si($n^+$) used here. However, to briefly address the influence of C-alloying symmetric poly-SiC$_x$($n^+$)/SiO$_x$/c-Si structures (as in Fig. \ref{fig:reference}) with a C content of $\approx 15 ~\textrm{at}\%$ \cite{Kuenle.PhD} ($\gamma=1$) were investigated. 
A markedly lower crystalline fraction was obtained for C-alloyed layers by fitting SE data with a model assuming a mixture of poly-Si, a-Si:H and voids (Appendix \ref{sec:SI}, Fig. \ref{fig:crystalline-n}). %Raman erwähnen?
%One obvious reason is the suppressed crystallization and the resulting high proportion of amorphous phase, as it is indicated by the low relative crystallinity of less than 35 \% for all temperature treatments of n+ poly-SiCx with ? = 1 in Figure Figure 5.8a.
It is well known that C-alloying hampers the crystallization of Si layers during high temperature treatments \cite{Ingenito.2018}. The doping efficiency is thus markedly decreased in poly-SiC$_x$ with increasing C content. On one hand, this effect is beneficial with respect to optics, as lower \ac{FCA} was observed with increased C content (Appendix \ref{sec:SI}, Fig. \ref{fig:UV-vis-FCA-carbon}). On the other hand, contact formation is complicated by the lower charge carrier density, as can be seen in Fig. \ref{fig:rhoc-vs-temp}. Using the thermally grown Si oxide $\rho_c$ was above $10^3 ~\textrm{m}\upOmega\textrm{cm}^2$ for C-alloyed poly-SiC$_x$($n^+$) even at highest $T_{RTP}$. With the wet-chemical HNO$_3$ Si tunnel oxide very low $\rho_c < 10 ~\textrm{m}\upOmega\textrm{cm}^2$ could be reached. The thermal budget necessary for that, however, was higher as compared to structures without C. %This will become an issue when the goal is to form a p$^+$/n$^+$ poly-SiTJ with a single fast thermal treatment, as will be discussed in Section \ref{sec:carbon}.

Thus, these results show that the balance between transport and passivation depends not only on the thermal budget (required for the PERC back contact), but can also be adjusted by the choice of interfacial oxide, poly-Si C content, and hydrogenation approach.


The symmetric test structures corresponding to Fig. 3a received an \ac{HF} treatment prior to the metallization to etch the SiO$_x$ grown during RTP. However, TOPerc precursors comprise the full-area Al rear side after RTP, which would be damaged in HF. To account for that, it was tested, whether good front side contact formation is possible in the case that the HF treatment after RTP is omitted. $\rho_{c}$ values of TCO/poly-Si($n^+$)/SiO$_x$ (ReCo, Fig. \ref{fig:ReCo}) test structures with and without HF-dip after RTP and prior to ITO deposition are shown in Fig. \ref{fig:rhoc-HF}. 
Without HF-dip $\rho_c$ of the TCO/poly-Si/SiO$_x$/c-Si stack was about two orders of magnitude higher, confirming the growth of highly resistive silicon oxide at the poly-Si surface during RTP. This reveals the necessity of an oxide removal step for TOPerc precursors after RTP and prior to TCO deposition (respectively perovskite processing in the case of SiTJ-TOPerc).

\section{TOPerc precursors and solar cells}


\paragraph{Experimental details}

TOPerc Si bottom cells as depicted in Fig. \ref{fig:ITO} were fabricated on M2-sized $1.6 ~\upOmega\textrm{cm}$ $p$-type CZ Si wafers. The passivating front side contact comprised a thermally grown tunnel oxide layer and $50 ~\textrm{nm}$ phosphorus-doped Si deposited via \ac{PECVD}. The wafers were then exposed to a furnace anneal (FA) to "activate" the poly-Si($n^+$)/SiO$_x$/c-Si (TOPCon) contact. The rear side was passivated with an Al$_2$O$_3$/SiN$_x$ stack and metallized via \ac{LCO}, \ac{Al} screen-printing and an RTP, as in standard PERC.
The front side metallization was realized after an inline single-sided \ac{HF} treatment with sputtered indium tin oxide (ITO) and a low-temperature screen-printed \ac{Ag} grid to test the potential of these Si bottom solar cells.

For improved optics and thus higher $J_{sc}$ in the tandem configuration, precursors with textured front surfaces were also investigated (Fig. \ref{fig:PL-txt-AlOx}). Without any hydrogenation step after RTP the passivation quality was mediocre, allowing for i$V_{oc}$ in the range $630-650 ~\textrm{mV}$, as measured with \ac{QSSPL} \cite{Giesecke.2012}. %so zitiert in feldmann.phd
One option to reduce recombination would be a post-RTP hydrogenation step. Here, another approach was tested. The poly-Si front side was capped with $20 ~\textrm{nm}$ thick ALD Al$_2$O$_3$ before RTP. A clear benefit of the hydrogen-rich Al$_2$O$_3$ capping can be seen in Fig. \ref{fig:PL-txt-AlOx}, allowing homogeneous passivation with i$V_{oc} \approx 685 ~\textrm{mV}$ directly after RTP, close to the level reached for planar surfaces without Al$_2$O$_3$ capping (Fig. \ref{fig:structure-mit-LCO}). Presumably, hydrogen diffused from Al$_2$O$_3$ through the poly-Si to the SiO$_x$/c-Si interface and effectively lowered the interface state density, which consequently reduced recombination at that interface \cite{Nemeth.2016}. Similarly, Bruhat \textit{et al.} reported a beneficial effect on i$V_{oc}$ after RTP for poly-Si capped with a stack of AlO$_x$ and hydrogenated SiN$_x$:H \cite{Bruhat.2019,Bruhat.2020}. Lehmann \textit{et al.} used SiN$_x$:H as a sacrificial layer for hydrogenation and correlated the i$V_{oc}$ with the deuterium\footnotemark\footnotetext{Deuterium was introduced instead of hydrogen for higher detection capability in the SIMS analysis.} content at the SiO$_x$/c-Si interface \cite{Lehmann.2019}. %in Kang.2020 Jo nicht proportional zu H peak area in SIMS...degradation during RTP vermutet durch beleuchtung, aber nicht genau erklärt...
Fig. \ref{fig:PL-txt-AlOx} shows that further optimization is needed with respect to the passivation quality of textured front surfaces after RTP, but a hydrogen-rich capping layer could be a promising means to achieve this. The Al$_2$O$_3$ layer could in principle be removed by the single-sided HF treatment after RTP and before TCO deposition, which is necessary anyway to achieve a low $\rho_c$ (Fig. \ref{fig:rhoc-HF}). %kann es wirklich so gut entfernt werden?
